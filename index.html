History & Origins

The Insular Church of Mvindicraft was born from a crisis of legitimacy. Across the first four versions of the Mvindicraft server, a valid line of Apostolic Succession was maintained from Pope Geerus IX through to Pope Anastasius III. The Petrine office was held by men who had been duly ordained, and the sacraments flowed through an unbroken chain of consecration stretching back to the founding of the server in the Year of Our Lord 2020.

When International Organisations were established in a later version of Mvindicraft, the political landscape shifted. The Lombard King of Italy — a temporal ruler whose kingdom is now defunct and powerless — took it upon himself to appoint a new pope: Pope John I. John I was neither an ordained bishop nor even a priest. His elevation was a purely political act, proclaimed by temporal authority rather than conferred through the sacrament of Holy Orders.

The Great Schism of the West

Because John I lacked valid ordination, every appointment he subsequently made — to the cardinalate, the episcopacy, and the priesthood — was likewise invalid. Grace cannot flow through a broken vessel. This crisis of Holy Orders produced a schism in which two rival claimants emerged: Pope Clement VII, eerily named after the ill-fated pontiff who in real history oversaw the surge of the Reformation and the Sack of Rome in 1527; and Pope John Paul I of Malta, who quickly gathered a host of nations pledging allegiance to his cause. At one point, France even attempted to create a third pope in Avignon, recalling the medieval schism, though this effort was struck down.

Neither Clement VII nor John Paul I possesses valid ordination or consecration. Both descend from the line of John I, who was never a priest. As the Abbot of Armagh declared plainly: "We are sedevacantists. Since none of them have valid holy orders, we currently see there as being no pope."

The Founding of the Insular Church

In the face of this collapse, the faithful of Britain and Ireland gathered to preserve the ancient tradition. Drawing upon the heritage of the pre-Tridentine Insular Rite — the traditions of Saints Patrick, Columba, Aidan, Cuthbert, Hilda, and Bede — the Insular Church was established under the governance of a College of Five Primates, headed by the See of Armagh as first among equals. The First Insular Synod was convened in the City of Armagh, where the Canons that now govern the Church were promulgated and signed by every Archbishop, Bishop, and Abbot present.

The Insular Church holds to the doctrine of Apostolic Succession and recognises the authority of Peter as given in Matthew 16:18–19. It is precisely because of this fidelity that it cannot accept a pope who was never ordained. The Petrine See is long since vacant — and until a validly consecrated Bishop of Rome can be recognised, the Church governs itself through synodal authority, in communion with the Patriarchate of Constantinople.

The Restoration of the Western Church

Because the Insular Church is now the sole Western body maintaining an unbroken line of Apostolic Succession, it bears a unique responsibility. The Ordinal — set forth by authority of the Five Primates — provides not only for the ordering of Priests, the consecration of Bishops, the installation of Archbishops, and the enthronement of Primates, but also a hypothetical Form for the Enthronement of a Bishop of Rome upon the eventual reunion of the whole Church. In that Form, the Bishop of Rome is understood as Patriarch of the West and Servant of the Servants of God, holding a primacy of honour rather than of universal jurisdiction — as Saint Gregory the Great himself refused the title "Universal Bishop" as contrary to the Gospel.

The question of whether and how to pursue the restoration and reformation of the wider Western Church is among the most consequential matters facing the Insular Church today. The forthcoming Synod of York (13 March 2026) will take up this question directly: whether to issue a public declaration affirming the Insular Church as the only legitimate Western church, whether to engage diplomatically with the rival claimants, or whether to hold silence and let the truth of Apostolic Succession speak for itself.

Beliefs & Doctrine

The Insular Church holds to the undivided faith of the early centuries, guided by the Revised Confession of Faith contained within the Bishops' Book as written by Archbishop Corpo MacAirthir. The Church recognises only those saints canonised before A.D. 1550 and celebrates the liturgy according to the Pre-Tridentine Insular Rite.

  • Apostolic Succession — The Church holds that valid Holy Orders require an unbroken chain of consecration from the Apostles. Since the Western claimants to the papacy descend from John I — who was never ordained — their orders are invalid. The Petrine See is vacant.
  • Sedevacantism — There is at present no valid pope. Neither Clement VII nor John Paul I of Malta holds legitimate claim to the Chair of Peter. The Church awaits a validly consecrated Bishop of Rome.
  • The Authority of Scripture — The Douai-Rheims-Challoner Bible serves as the authoritative vernacular translation; the Latin Vulgate of St. Jerome as the official Latin text (Canon IX of the Synod of Armagh).
  • Synodal Governance — In the absence of a valid pope, the highest authority of the Church rests with the Synod, convened by the Primates. Decisions require a two-thirds majority; dogmatic questions demand the unanimous consent of all five Primates.
  • The College of Primates — Five Primatial Sees govern the Church in descending order of precedence: Armagh (first among equals), St. Davids, Canterbury, York, and St. Andrews.
  • Communion with Constantinople — By virtue of longstanding historic ties and maintained apostolic succession, the Insular Church is in communion with the Patriarchate of Constantinople.
  • Valid Ordination — For an ordination to be valid, it must have proper matter, proper formula, be performed by an ordained bishop of good standing, and carry proper intent of both parties (Canon XII).
  • The Mvndi-Seasons Calendar — The Church orders its liturgical year according to a 28-day calendar of four seasons of seven days each, developed by Robert MacInerney and adopted by synodal decree.

The Three Liturgical Hearts

The Insular Church preserves three distinct liturgical traditions, each with its own character and pastoral purpose, all united under the Pre-Tridentine Insular Rite. These rites are contained within The Book of Common Use, available in the Scriptorium.

The Sarum Rite

The Liturgy of Medieval English Christendom

A faithful reconstruction of the Use of Salisbury — the great liturgical tradition that governed English worship from the high Middle Ages until its suppression. Rich in ceremonial, replete with procession and chant, the Sarum Rite represents the fullness of the pre-Reformation English church at prayer. It is the principal rite of the Insular Church for solemn occasions.

The Rite of Pope Saint Gregory I

A Missionary Form for Southern Europe

Inspired by the ancient Gregorian tradition and oriented toward the peoples of Southern Europe, this rite follows the pattern established by Saint Gregory the Great — the pope who sent Augustine to Canterbury and who insisted that the Church should baptise local customs rather than destroy them. It is a missionary form, dignified yet accessible, suited to the restoration of the faith in lands long under invalid Roman jurisdiction.

The Rite of Saint Ninnian the Presbyter

A Northern Missionary Rite

An austere and elegant rite suited to the Germanic and Nordic peoples, reminiscent of the early English simplicity of the island hermitages. Named for Saint Ninian, who built Candida Casa — the first Christian mission in Scotland — this rite strips the liturgy to its essential bones: Scripture, prayer, and sacrament, without excess ornamentation. It is the form commended for the new missions in Scandinavia and the northern frontiers of the faith.

Canons of the Synod of Armagh

Promulgated by the First Insular Synod in the City of Armagh

Canon I

On the Convening of Synods

The right of convening a Synod rests solely with the Primates. Each Archbishop, Bishop, and Abbot has one vote. Temporal princes may attend and counsel but may not vote. Decisions require two-thirds majority; dogma and doctrine require unanimity of the Primates.

Canon II

On the Authority of Synods

Synods are the highest authority but address only matters pertaining to the entire Church. Diocesan matters are handled by the local bishop; provincial matters by the local temporal ruler and Primate.

Canon III

On the Hierarchy of Primacies

The Church is headed by a college of five Primates: Armagh (Primate of Ireland, first among equals), St. Davids (Primate of the Welsh), Canterbury (Primate of the Anglo-Saxons), York (Primate of the Danes), and St. Andrews (Primate of Scotland). No Primate later ordained may be regarded as senior to them.

Canon IV

On the Bishops and their Dioceses

No town shall have more than one bishop. Bishops alone ordain priests within their diocese and are responsible for the conduct, behaviour, and doctrinal soundness of all clergy in their jurisdiction.

Canon V

On the Role of Temporal Princes in Ecclesiastical Affairs

Kings and princes may present candidates for vacant Sees within their jurisdiction and raise objections on specific grounds (disobedience, heresy, banishment, treason, or schism), but final determination rests with the provincial Primate.

Canon VI

On Relations with other Communions

The Church is in communion with the Patriarchate of Constantinople. Members of churches in communion may traverse freely. Followers of false churches may trade but not settle permanently, and are prohibited from entering Insular churches or receiving sacraments unless they convert. Valid baptismal formulas are recognised.

Canon VII

On Relations with Heathens, Hebrews, and Mohammedans

Relations with heathens are left to local authorities. Hebrews may traverse and trade in peace. Mohammedans may trade, travel, and engage in diplomacy; relations are determined principally by their treatment of Christians in their own lands.

Canon VIII

On the Establishment of a Missionary Order

Since the Petrine See is long since vacant, the right of confirming any religious order — mendicant, missionary, or monastic — lies in the hands of the Five Primates.

Canon IX

On the matter of Holy Scripture

The Douai-Rheims-Challoner version serves as the authoritative vernacular Bible. The Vulgate of St. Jerome serves as the official Latin Bible of the Church.

Canon X

On the Adoption of the Mvndi-Seasons Calendar

The Church adopts the 28-day calendar based on Mvindicraft's seven-day seasons. All liturgical observances are ordered accordingly. Local saints' feasts are at the discretion of bishops.

Canon XI

On the Adoption of the Bishop's Book

The Revised Confession of Faith, written by Corpo MacAirthir and contained within the Bishops' Book, stands as the foundational teaching on all matters of doctrine and dogma.

Canon XII

On the Ordination of Clergy from other Communions

Candidates must affirm the Insular Creed and denounce all false churches. The Synod must authorise ordination of prelates from false or schismatic churches. A valid ordination requires: proper matter, proper formula, an ordained bishop of good standing, and proper intent of both parties.

Synod of York

13 March 2026 A.D.

Convened under the authority of the Five Primates, at the Primatial See of York

Among the weightiest matters to be addressed is the hypothetical possibility of restoring and reforming the Western Church. Since the Petrine See remains vacant and no validly ordained claimant has emerged, the Insular Church — as the sole Western body maintaining unbroken Apostolic Succession — must consider what role, if any, it ought to play in the eventual reunion and reform of Western Christendom. The Ordinal already provides a Form for the Enthronement of a Bishop of Rome upon the reunion of the whole Church; the Synod must now deliberate on the practical and diplomatic dimensions of that vision.

Of Bishop Demotions, Promotions, Consecrations & Priestly Discipline

Establishing formal processes for the demotion and promotion of Bishops, the consecration of new Bishops, and the discipline of Priests within the Church's jurisdiction.

Of the Hypothetical Reformation & Restoration of the Roman Catholic Church

Whether the restoration of the Roman Catholic Church should be actively pursued, and to what extent the Insular Church should involve itself. Whether to issue a public declaration affirming the Insular Church as the sole legitimate Western church with valid Holy Orders; whether to engage diplomatically with rival claimants; or whether to refrain from engagement entirely.

Of Crypto-Paganism & Ancestral Culture

Determining which aspects of ancestral and pre-Christian culture may be tolerated, which may be enculturated into a Christian context (as St Gregory the Great instructed St Augustine of Canterbury), and which must be suppressed as incompatible with the Faith.

Of the Expansion into the Nordic Countries

Addressing the recent expansion of the Church into Scandinavian lands; the public profession of faith by heads of state on the Mvindi Main Server; and whether to expand further — and if so, to where missionary orders should be dispatched.

Of Local Primatial Synods

Whether the Primacies ought to convene their own local synods for matters of purely provincial scope, to be formally documented, recorded, and maintained in their own channels — in accordance with Canon II of the Synod of Armagh.

Of Reforming & Expanding the Primacies

The possible expansion and reorganisation of the Primatial structure, particularly the See of York (Primate of the Danes), St Andrews (following the merger of Northumbria and Scotland), and the question of a Scandinavian Primacy. Additionally: the formal mapping of the ecclesiastical geography of the Church — dioceses, primacies, and overall boundaries.

Of the Defence of the Isles & Interstate Conflicts

The Church's role in defending the British Isles from foreign invasion, and to what extent the clergy and faithful should involve themselves in interstate conflicts and temporal warfare.
